202 202 VOLUME 202

In terms of Standing Order No. 126, the COPE is required to present its Reports to the House. These Reports are under preparation and it is proposed to place them before the House before the Budget Debate. I owe the House an explanation as to the delay.

As you are well aware, Mr. Deputy Speaker, the COPE commenced examining the Audit Reports relating to the institutions assigned to it as from 4th August, 2010. The COPE elected by the Seventh Parliament has been able to clear a backlog of arrears covering several outstanding years of accounts, bringing the work of the COPE up to date.

As from January, 2012, we shall be concentrating on the accounts for the year 2010 and perhaps 2011. Now we are in a position to devote more time on the performance of those institutions and also on the current activities.

of Labour, to the Labour Tribunal or to an industrial arbitration, and that there is a substantial sum that they would be afforded which is in real terms a compensation. I entirely agree with the Hon. Minister of National Languages and Social Integration when he says that the workman compensation formula also needs to be revisited. It is grossly inadequate and those are some of the things that need to be done.

With regard to the certainty of the law, there were certain remarks made by the Hon. Member for Gampaha from the Opposition and I would also like to make certain remarks in relation to that. Today we have before us a gazette placed on the table on the appointment of the Commissioner-General of Rehabilitation made on the 25th of August, 2011 under Emergency Regulations. Now this is very strange. This appointment has been made under the provisions of Emergency Regulation 22 of the Public Security Ordinance after His Excellency the President announced in this House that there would be no more Emergency Regulations and that he is satisfied that conditions for the state of Emergency are not necessary. Now after he made such a solemn statement in this House, he himself has signed and published a gazette under Emergency Regulation 22. Now this is another example of uncertainty with regard to the rule of law.

Several statements are made. This is not necessary. But then it is brought through some other back-channel. We are about to see the spectacle of all of the Emergency Regulations being brought through the back-channel of the Prevention of Terrorism Act.

As it stands today, Hon. Presiding Member, I would like to draw your attention to the fact that four gazette notifications, dated 29th of August have been titled, only titled, but not printed or published. It is a very serious issue. Eight days have passed since the 29th of August and we do not have them on our table. They have not been tabled in this House. There are said to be regulations which are really not there. If one goes into the website of the Government Publications Bureau you cannot see those gazettes. You can only see the title and the number. I am making a very serious allegation now that the Government has held out that four Regulations have been made when, in fact, none have been made. The date, the number and the title to these gazette notifications have been given without there being in existence any such gazette. This is a very, very serious issue with regard to the rule of law. I hope tomorrow when the Government moves the Adjournment Motion, this matter will be debated as well.

Now with regard to certainty, law must be certain; citizens must know what the law is. That is the issue with regard to the workmen as well. The matter before us today is increasing the penalty. As I said right at the beginning I support this, because we must have an effective mechanism to enforce these orders that are in favour of the workmen. But I ask this question - how
