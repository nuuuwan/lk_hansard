SET OF GUIDELINES FOR ADJOURNMENT QUESTIONS. SET OF GUIDELINES FOR ADJOURNMENT QUESTIONS.

1. One hour shall be allocated for Adjournment Questions on the allotted day, and six Adjournment Questions shall only be allowed, 3 for the Government and 3 for the Opposition proportionately.

If either the Government or the Opposition do not submit the required number of Adjournment Questions for any such day, any additional Questions submitted by the Government or the Opposition, as the case maybe, will be accommodated.

2. Adjournment Questions shall be restricted to matters of urgent public importance and a Member of Parliament shall be allowed to ask only one such Question per day.

Matter of urgent public importance of Adjournment Questions shall be decided by Mr. Speaker.

3. Members of Parliament could submit the Adjournment Questions to their respective Chief Whips through fax mode provided that the Question has been signed by the Member of Parliament concerned.

The Respective Chief Whips shall submit such Questions to the Secretary-General of Parliament by 10.00 a.m. of the preceding day and by 12.00 noon, the Adjournment Questions shall be sent to the Leader of the House of Parliament.

4. The Leader of the House of Parliament shall submit the Questions to the respective Ministers to answer. The Ministers will have the right to answer or not answer a Question, and to request on the Floor of the House that it be listed as a Question for oral answers, if necessary.

5. A Member of Parliament asking an Adjournment Question should confine himself only to the Question for which notice has already been given.

6. No Supplementary Questions to an Adjournment Question shall be allowed.

7. Provisions of the Rules under the Standing Order No. 31 (1) - (10) shall apply to the Adjournment Questions as well. ( ) (The Hon. Buddhika Pathirana) , . .

FORMULATION OF NEW LAWS TO PREVENT CRUELTY TO ANIMALS ( ) (The Hon. Lakshman Kiriella) , :

" ." ( ) (The Presiding Member) , .

. ( . ) (The Hon. M. Joseph Michael Perera) , .

( ) (The Hon. Lakshman Kiriella) Mr. Presiding Member, this Adjournment Motion is being moved to bring to light the plight of animals and basically, what we call cruelty to animals. The purpose of this Adjournment Motion is to bring to the notice of Parliament that there are inadequate laws and there is no

public awareness with regard to this problem. Though Sri Lanka is a Buddhist country, it is unfortunate that this problem of cruelty to animals is not very much focused on in Sri Lanka.

Cruelty to animals takes many forms: caging, being held in captivity, inadequate sustenance, slaughter and so on. So, I think it is time that Members of Parliament take note of this problem and bring in adequate legislation. Caging animals to a lifetime of captivity in a zoo, a pet shop or in a home, is a sinful or cruel act that we should be ashamed of. Big cats, eagles and other animals travel long distances daily when living free. Caging them for the pleasure of a few, while subjecting these helpless animals to a lifetime of misery, is sinful. Every animal, from the mightiest to the tiniest which crawl at our feet, has the right to live free. Can we, as humans who can think, be contended to be in a similar situation as a caged animal?

Animals are not a creation of a supernatural for the pleasure of man. They are also a part of the ecosystem as the human beings. Therefore, their right to live free should be respected. Much criticism has been levelled against zoos and pet shops periodically with regard to starvation, unhygienic conditions, insufficient space and it is time that the Legislature enact laws to dismantle that. I would like to quote Jawaharlal Nehru:

Wildlife? That is how we refer to the magnificent animals of our jungles and to the beautiful birds that brighten our lives. I wonder sometimes what these animals and birds think of man and how they would describe him, if they had the capacity to do so. I rather doubt, if their description would be very complimentary to man.

That is what the Prime Minister of India, Jawaharlal Nehru said many years ago.

Sir, in most countries, today they are closing down zoos; it is happening in most countries. I do not have the time to read out the entire list of such countries that I have. The United States is gradually phasing out zoos in their respective states. Some of the zoos are Detroit Zoo in Michigan, Philadelphia Zoo in Pennsylvania, the London Zoo, the Edinburgh Zoo and the Bristol Zoo in the United Kingdom, Alaska Zoo in Alaska, Lincoln Park Zoo in Chicago, Greater Vancouver Zoo in British Columbia, Chehaw Wild Animal Park in Georgia, San Francisco Zoo in California and Gladys Porter Zoo in Texas. The actual figure is much more than this. But, the principle is that most countries have accepted the fact that zoos should be phased out gradually and that animals should live in their natural habitats.

So much so, that the Central Zoo Authority of India issued a directive in 2009 stating that all 140 elephants living in the Indian Zoos must be moved to wildlife parks and sanctuaries where they can move freely. So, as far as we are concerned, we are far behind. This type of legislation was absent in our country, maybe because we

125 126

were involved in other more serious problems like the thirty-year-old war. Maybe, it is time now for us to think about this issue. I would like to quote from the editorial of The Island newspaper of 26th December, 2013. It states, I quote:

Why a female anaconda swallowed her mate.

They do not have food. There are several instances like this. This was in 2013; just one year ago. According to this editorial, animals in the zoo do not have adequate food. The wildlife enthusiasts tried to contact the Director of the Zoo, but they could not. So, we have to think of this seriously. I have another article with me which states: Costa Rica set to close public zoos and release the animals back into the wild.

Many countries are following this. In advanced countries there is a limiting of sound intensity of firecrackers. , . Lighting of unrestricted firecrackers during festive seasons and elections cause panic, fear and confusion among animals. In Sri Lanka, we do not have laws to control that. I would like to table* this document relating to this issue.

In the United Kingdom, there are laws which control the intensity of fireworks. In the UK, fireworks over 120 decibels at 15 metres cannot be sold to the public. The Threshold of Pain for intensity varies from 120 - 130 decibels among different individuals. So, the question is, what are we going to do?

The fact of the matter is that there are not enough laws. If you go to a pet shop, you will see massive birds inside very small cages. So much so, that you will not believe, sometimes the wings of the birds are pinned to the body so that even if the birds come out of the cage by some means or other, they could not escape. So many animal lovers have told me that this is the situation.

With regard to breeding of dogs, they have one round of breeding and then in a few months, they are given artificial hormones and chemicals and are made to breed again. This cycle goes on almost non stop. I have a statement made by the senior environmentalist and environmental lawyer, Mr. Jagath Gunawardena. He says, I quote:

There are several laws that can be made use of including the Prevention of Cruelty to Animals Ordinance, No. 13 of 1907 and the Animal Diseases Act to take action against animal cruelty but they are all concerning action that can be taken once an incident is reported.

Once an incident is reported only, you can take action under the present laws.

There are not enough laws to prevent cruelty to animals. So, Parliament has to look into this and enact laws to prevent cruelty to animals. Now the law is, after a certain act is reported only, the Police can intervene.

Sir, before the Commonwealth Heads of Government Meeting, many stray dogs were rounded up. In fact, this morning a lady telephoned me after she came to know that this Debate is going to be taken up today in Parliament. She said that not only before the CHOGM but before any conference, stray dogs are rounded up. There was a time when they rounded up only beggars, but now they have started rounding up even the dogs. So, this has to be stopped. I think my Friend, the Hon. Nimal Siripala de Silvas beloved wife is also a great animal lover. I remember during the CHOGM, she tried her best to safeguard the lives of stray dogs.

I will conclude soon because the Hon. Ajith P. Perera also has to speak. Basically these are the shortcomings of the present laws: lack of broader coverage of animal welfare issues; protection of animals limited to animals, domestic or captured, and animals in captivity; limited number of animal cruelty offences; lack of regulation of animals in scientific research; transport of animals; and exhibition and training of performing animals. You know animals are being used in circuses and so on, and breeding of animals. As I told you, the owners of pet shops make animals breed continuously so that they become inferior in their genetic composition. Next, inadequate power of the police to enforce the law; inadequate period of time for making a police complaint; low monetary penalties - the penalties are very low. Mr. Presiding Member, the most important matter is that there is an absence of a proper authority to administer legislation.

So, I will conclude my speech saying that Sri Lanka being a Buddhist country should concentrate more on this subject and I hope the Government will take notice of these protestations and bring in laws to safeguard the animals.

Thank you. . ( . ) (The Hon. Ajith P. Perera) , . . . . . , .
