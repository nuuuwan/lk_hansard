(Dr.) Sarath Amunugama earlier mentioned in this House, that we are given an agenda and that we are supposed to speak on the given topic, not about everyday political problems, without throwing mud at each other. I appreciate that comment, because if we are not able to understand the scope of the Debate, then we will be talking unnecessary and useless things in this House and the quality of the Debate will continue to deteriorate. So, I acknowledge and endorse what the Hon. Member said.

Today, we are debating the Amendment to the Chemical Weapons Convention Act. In fact, it is only about the transfer of the administration of this Convention from the Ministry of Industries to the Ministry of Defence. The President had suggested that it should be taken under the Ministry of Defence because it is a matter of national importance. Certainly it is. It is not about, as the Hon. (Mrs.) Pavithradevi Wanniarachchi mentioned, face creams. It is about blister agents, nerve agents, choking agents, blood control agents and so on. And, like I said earlier, here we are not talking about, as some others spoke about, mustard gas, sarin gas, chlorine, hydrogen gas, cyanide and so on. In warfare today, countries use chemical weapons in order to combat various State and non-State parties waging wars against governments.

We have heard stories about hundreds of thousands of people losing their lives during the First World War due to chemical weapons. But, in fact, during the Second World War, chemical weapons were not commonly used. However, what is happening today is something of great concern, particularly in the case of Syria. There have been multiple allegations that in Syria, under President Bashar al-Assads leadership, chemical weapons are being used on groups fighting against the Government. As the Hon. (Dr.) Sarath Amunugama mentioned, it is well known that Russia has been continuously defending

That is about cluster munitions. I was the Deputy Minister of Foreign Affairs when we were discussing this in international fora where we were one of the very few countries in the world who had not become a State party to ban cluster munitions. Why can we not ban cluster munitions in this country? Why do you need cluster bombing? No one needs to cluster bomb anyone. These methods of warfare have been taken away, because like the Hon. (Dr.) Amunugama said, it is impacting not only on the person shooting at you but the bigger population. So, that is why these have become weapons of mass destruction - because if you drop a cluster bomb somewhere you kill not two terrorists but you kill 200 people in the entire village. Therefore, I am extremely glad and I congratulate the Government because in March, 2018 Sri Lanka finally signed up as the 103rd country - one of the last countries - on the Convention on Cluster Munitions. What that also makes us do is to transparently let the Convention on Cluster Munitions to examine our non-use of cluster munitions in any sort of instance. So, the use, stockpiling, clearing and destruction of any remaining cluster munitions is now the responsibility of the Government of Sri Lanka.
