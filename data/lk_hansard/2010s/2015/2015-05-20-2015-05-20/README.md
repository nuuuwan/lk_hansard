Sir, I would explain that in English.

The passport-issuing function is carried out by the Department of Immigration and Emigration - DIE. Since commissioning the computerized passport-issuing process in August 2003, the DIE has issued over five million machine-readable passports. On an average, the Department issues approximately 3,000 passports per day.

Over the years, the Department has encountered increasing challenges in maintaining the quality and the reliability of the passport. The challenges largely emanated from two areas: one, is the quality of the passport booklet, and the second, is the integrity in the passport-issuing process. These are the deficiencies that were discovered over the years.

In order to improve the quality of the passport booklet, the Department in line with international standards, has already introduced many advanced security features to the passport, making it tamper-proof. The idea is to ensure that there is no tampering. However, gaps in the issuing process, in particular, the establishment of identity of applicants and verifying the authenticity of the cardinal documents remain a considerable challenge to date. At the same time, establishment of identity only on verification of cardinal documents proved to be difficult. Due to these gaps, the chances of one person obtaining multiple passports under different identities remain a possibility. We had this experience, Sir, and some incidents were also revealed in the House as to how two passports have been obtained. The issuing of passports to the required international standards has, therefore, become a challenging task for the Department.

Having realized the need for managing these challenges, the Department in 2006, requested the International Organization for Migration - IOM, to conduct a risk assessment on the passport-issuing process. A study, among others, has recognized that the establishment of identity in the issuing process is a serious challenge and recommended the use of fingerprints to eliminate instances of multiple identities.

After conducting a feasibility study, the Department has accepted the recommendation to introduce fingerprints to the application-approval process and the Government of Australia has pledged to provide a grant through the IOM to implement the recommendation. The Cabinet of Ministers, on 29th August, 2013, by Cabinet Paper No. 13/1191/503/094, has approved the project to be implemented with an Australian grant. We are thankful to the Government of Australia for having come forward to give financial assistance to improve this project.

An Amendment to the Immigrants and Emigrants Act has been prepared by the Legal Draftsman to obtain fingerprints from passport applicants. With the necessary approval from the Attorney-General, the Amendment has been published in the Government Gazette and the Amendment to the Act is submitted to Parliament for approval.

It is well-known that fingerprints are the best method, world over, to identify people - whether it be criminal or any other aspect - as there are no two persons who have identical fingerprints. So, I think this is a very important Amendment and, soon, we will be issuing a new passport including all these features, and this fingerprinting process will start immediately once this House approves this Amendment. Thank you.

Deputy Chairman of Committees, today everyone has been speaking about the incident that took place in Kayts, where a young girl who was on her way to school was taken away, raped and murdered. The incident itself is very saddening. It is an incident that must be condemned. But, rape and murder have been taking place all over the country. Today, in the Jaffna Peninsula, there is a protest; there is a lot of agitation; people are angry and they are taking the law into their hands.

The Hon. Keheliya Rambukwella, in this House, requested the Hon. Minister to act immediately to bring the situation in Jaffna under control. It is unfortunate that this situation has developed and the people in Jaffna are blaming the police. My Colleague, the Hon. (Mrs.) Vijayakala Maheswaran, when she spoke was so agitated and said that the police did not take action. She even mentioned that one of the rapists was caught by the people, tied to a pillar, held, and several messages were passed to the police but the police did not turn up to arrest him. The people around had assaulted him badly but still he had escaped and come to Colombo and had been later arrested here in Colombo. They demand that he be brought back to Jaffna and be produced in court. They demand that severe action be taken against the nine rapists. There still remains one more person who had stood guard and who has not been arrested. This reflects very badly on the police.

I have, in the past, pointed out several incidents in this House, where the police have not acted immediately, or due to actions of the police, certain things have happened. I mentioned the incident in Ratnapura where a woman was assaulted in public by a police officer in uniform. I

105 106

raised this with the IGP also. He brushed it aside and said that she had provoked him. However much a civilian provokes, a police officer cannot be assaulting a woman in that manner in public. No action was taken; he was reinstated; he has resumed work and probably he may be continuing to do so, though the incident was recorded on a mobile phone.

Similarly, I raised an issue regarding action not being taken against the policeman who chased a young boy in Talawakele, who, in fear of the policeman, jumped into the pond of the Upper Kotmale Hydropower Project and died. I raised this issue directly with the IGP in the presence of the Hon. Minister, but he wrote back to me saying that it was a mere suicide. Subsequently, another young person in the Anuradhapura District, who was also under duress of the police, was forced to commit suicide and no action has been taken against the police officials. We have also heard a few years ago about a mentally-retarded boy being chased into the sea on the beach in Bambalapitiya and then being pushed to such a level, that he drowned and died in the sea. So, the police is not acting in a proper manner. The IGP is totally ignoring the actions of the police and he thinks that the police is superior to all others in this country. This is very condemnable and the police should take responsibility for whatever is happening today in the Northern Peninsula.

The Hon. Minister of Public Order and Christian Religious Affairs has, today, brought in an Amendment to the Immigrants and Emigrants Act, which provides for the public to be fingerprinted for the purpose of issuing a passport. In the past, when foreign embassies introduced a system of fingerprinting persons when applying for visas to their countries, there was a big protest in this country against that. Subsequently, we all gave in because this has become a scientific requirement to have biometric information and fingerprinting, which cannot be duplicated. It is the best way to identify a person holding a passport. So, we need not wait for foreign countries to insist on introducing biometric or fingerprinted passports to our country. It will be good and it is correct to go forward to introduce such security measures in issuing passports, and that passport will have more value and more respect all over the world.

Today, while I was presiding in this House, the Hon. Vinayagamoorthi Muralidaran commended this procedure of introducing the fingerprinted passports. If we had this law five years ago, he would not have been able to enter Britain on a forged passport. He has forgotten that and I am sure, in the future, he will not be able to do it. But, he also commends this process, which is quite shocking.

The new Government took a very bold and right decision to reintroduce the dual citizenship for those who have relinquished Sri Lankan citizenship due to obtaining

a foreign citizenship or for those who wish to retain Sri Lankan citizenship, while obtaining foreign citizenship. This is a very good move that the Government has taken within the Hundred-Day Programme because there was so much of agitation against the ban on dual citizenship.

I remember the Department of Immigration and Emigration came before the Committee on Public Accounts and the Auditor-General raised a query as to how, while dual citizenship was banned, over 3,000 people had been issued with dual citizenship and a revenue recorded by the Department of Immigration and Emigration. This ban was for those that the previous Government did not like; it was not banned for friends of the previous Government. So, in this country, before the 8th of January this year, we had a Government which was bias, racial and acting against the law. They did not treat all citizens as equals; they did not treat all those who wish to become dual citizens equally, but they treated their own friends and family in a different manner. Earlier, we had dual citizens only as Members of Parliament, Ministers and Secretaries to Ministries. But, today, we have opened up dual citizenship to everyone.

Also, under the Nineteenth Amendment, we have stopped those with dual citizenship from contesting elections. Today, there is a question mark as to whether those who hold dual citizenship will be entitled to vote in this country. The Nineteenth Amendment itself is not very clear on this issue and we have to get a clarification from the Government - from the Hon. Minister of Justice and the Attorney-General - as to whether dual citizens will be entitled to voting rights in this country and to contest, or whether they are only banned from contesting elections but, are entitled to vote. This is something that is agitating the people who have applied and got dual citizenship and there are a large number of people who, after the war, were in fear of coming back to this country, but are now, after the new Government took office, thinking of coming back and settling down. So, if they do come and settle down and contribute to the economy of this country, they would expect to have voting rights. As such, I think the right to vote should be granted to dual citizens while, maybe, politically it will be right for not allowing them to contest in this country.

The National Identity Card is also going to be made electronic. But, I also have a fear about how quickly and efficiently it will be done. We know that the National Identity Card system was brought in, in 1973 and it is now 42 years since it was introduced. But, up to date, the people in the plantations are having issues about the National Identity Cards because they do not have birth certificates.

The cause for the non-availability of birth certificates is that the plantation managers, who were supposed to register the births, could not do that on time. As such, today, some of them are still facing difficulties in
