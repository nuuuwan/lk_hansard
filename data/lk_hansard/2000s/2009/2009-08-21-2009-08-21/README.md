( . - ) (The Hon. V. Puththirasigamoney - Deputy Minister of Justice) Thank you, Mr. Deputy Chairman of Committees. It is a pleasure to speak after the Hon. Minister of Labour Relations and Manpower and the former Deputy Minister of Labour. Both of them have done something important pertaining to the EPF and the ETF. We are supportive of this piece of legislation brought in today by the Hon. Minister of Labour Relations and Manpower. I represent the plantation workers and I believe they are the single largest contributor to the EPF and the ETF, because the plantation industry is one of the biggest industries that we have in our country. In the meantime, we have some problems with regard to the refund of the benefits. The main problem is when some people move from one estate to another, they get a different membership number. We have been discussing this matter with the Hon. Minister. He has now agreed to give a single membership number for these people whenever they move. So, that is one of the important things. After the Employees Provident Fund was established in 1958, one of the sectors that benefited from the EPF is definitely the plantation sector, because before that there was no saving scheme for these people. Even now, the plantation workers do not have a habit of saving. The only saving they have is the EPF and the ETF. I also must say that even the staff officers of the plantations do not have a saving scheme except the EPF and the ETF. Here, we are speaking of a private pension scheme and a private fund. One of the well-maintained and well-administered funds in the estates is the Estate Staff's Provident Fund. This is one of the best funds we have. I believe in Sri Lanka, the Central Bank, Bank of Ceylon and the other banks have both the EPF, ETF and also a pension scheme. I believe that the plantation workers also should have a pension scheme as well, because when some of these plantation workers retire, they get a lump sum and their children take over that money. Then, these people become destitute. You can see some of them on the roads in the plantation areas such as Nuwara Eliya, Hatton, Kandy and Gampola. In those areas, you can see old people on the roads because they do not have any kind of a home for the aged or any other institution to look after them. So, they become destitute. The EPF and the ETF itself should establish some kind of a home for the aged that will provide shelter for the people who have been contributing to the EPF and the ETF for a number of years. Otherwise, after they retire, they become destitute. Mr. Deputy Chairman, you are also from a plantation area and you know once the plantation people get their EPF money, how they spend it. Sometimes, they go and buy all the luxury household goods and their youngsters go and buy three-wheelers or something like that for them

and then, they do not look after their parents. That is one of the biggest problems that we are facing in the plantation sector. So, this piece of legislation gives way for a private provident fund or a private pension scheme. I feel that we do not have a proper monitoring system. In general, the Department of Labour looks after all these private funds including the EPF contributors. But, when you look at most of these private small-time entrepreneurs, they do not contribute to the EPF and the ETF. I do not think that they have enough staff to go into various areas and look after these people. Say for instance, the workers who are working in haircutting saloons, small boutiques or small restaurants do not contribute to the EPF. Even people who run small industries do not contribute to the EPF and the ETF because they do not have adequate staff to look into all this. Some of the private estates do not even send their contributions to the EPF on time. Even some companies - I can mention one or two names, but I do not think it is very necessary - do not send their contributions to the EPF on time. They are not punished for this though there is provision in the law. I think the Hon. Minister and his officers who are here today will look into these areas and take appropriate action. And, if we do not have monetary regulations governing the private funds, when there is an economic crisis, people may use that money for certain other things and at the end of the day, the workers who contribute to such funds will suffer. From 1958, the contribution by both the employees and employers to the EPF and ETF have gone up by 25 per cent. That is a very significant improvement. The Hon. Minister also mentioned today that he will try to bring in an insurance scheme. We support that because as the Hon. Joseph Michael Perera pointed out, an insurance scheme is very, very necessary today for the people to cover their medical expenses. I know that the Estate Staff's Provident Fund has this provision. They give money to their members for medication, hospitalization and they give other benefits also. Since, we have a large amount of money in these funds, it can be used to cover the educational expenses of the members. In this regard, the Hon. Joseph Michael Perera came out with some novel ideas and I also agree with him. When the employees want to go abroad for study, we should be able to pay them out of the Employees Provident Fund and the ETF and give them all other benefits. While endorsing all what the Hon. Minister and also the former Minister of Labour said, we are very happy that this piece of legislation is put before the House and we are supportive of that. Thank you. [. . 11.35] ( ) (The Hon.
