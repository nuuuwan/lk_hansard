Bismillahir Rahmanir Raheem. Venerable Presiding Member, I thank you for giving me this opportunity to speak on the Supplementary Estimates sought for by the Ministry of Agricultural Development and Agrarian Services. Since I represent an agricultural community, I feel it is my duty to express a few views on the fertilizer subsidy that is in existence today. We all know that Sri Lanka is an agricultural country with diverse agro-climate and land resources. Sri Lanka has been identified to have 13 soil groups. The major soil types in Sri Lanka are Lateritic soils, the Red Earths and the Alluviums which basically constitute 90 per cent of Sri Lankas soil. Now, having this diversity in the soil that we call our own, we have rice as our major agricultural crop which has been grown for over 2000 years. I believe we have around 730,000 to 740,000 hectares under rice cultivation and I come from an area which is considered the rice bowl of Sri Lanka, the Digamadulla District. Having monitored or having observed the implementation of the fertilizer subsidy for the last two seasons, I find that we need to look at the operation of this subsidy much more carefully than what we are doing now because this subsidy is being given to increase the productivity of paddy in this country. I am not sure whether this subsidy has increased the yield per acre of paddy in this country. It may have helped a lot of people who were in difficulty in cultivating their lands to

get to growing rice and therefore increasing the rice production in this country. But, what we need to see also is whether there is a direct relationship to the increase in yield per acre by the subsidy that we are giving. Now, I explained that we have 13 different types of soil. But, we have one flat formula for the subsidy. We have Urea 125 kilograms per acre, Muriate of Potash (MOP) 45 kilograms per acre and Triple Super Phosphate (TSP) 35 kilograms per acre. If this is the prescribed quantity and if this is what is recommended for usage per acre, I beg to ask the question, how is it that a fair percentage of this fertilizer finds its way into the black market? If this is the required quantity of fertilizer to be used per acre to get the maximum yield, how is it that we find reports in the newspapers that there is a big racket in the fertilizer subsidy. Is it because the officers are juggling figures and getting more fertilizer into the districts than what is required for the acres cultivated or is it that the recommended dosage is high and therefore the farmers will not hesitate to give that extra quantity back to enter into the black market? Now, this is something that has to be studied and controlled effectively because when we have almost a 9 per cent negative growth in revenue performance for the first five or six months of the year, we cannot continue to increase the subsidies because it has to be paid in another way. Now, what you subsidize, on the other side you have to create extra revenue to cover that subsidy or you have to borrow. When you borrow, you have to pay back. When you have to pay back and if you cannot generate the revenue, you have to start taxing the people. So, you subsidize a portion of the community and you tax everybody else to cover that subsidy. I am not against the fertilizer subsidy. But, I am against the way in which these subsidies are doled out because when it is a subsidy everybody takes it, Oh! This is free. The Government is giving us a concession and what happens? That concession does not go to the people who deserve it. Who makes money in the meantime? It is the people involved in the administration of that subsidy who make the money. I am aware of so many corrupt officials who have amassed wealth after this fertilizer subsidy came into effect. I was told that last season alone, there were over 160 complaints of fraudulent activities on this subsidy. Now, this has to be nipped in the bud because what we do not realize is when we give these things free, the natural tendency is to use more than what is required. I believe the urea usage in the Digamadulla District is too high, the repercussions of which we may see in the years to come. How much of effect is it going to have on the soil? I am no expert. How much of effect or how much of contamination this is going to cause to the water that is being held under the soil we do not know because we are not looking at those things. So, we need to understand that anything that we do has to be controlled and co-ordinated and when it comes to co-ordination, I have complained before in my speeches in this House, that there is very little co-ordination between the Agrarian Services Department and the Agricultural Department. Everybody is working in compartments.

Now, with regard to this fertilizer, I know there was a problem during the last season in the delivery of fertilizer on time to the Ampara District. But, I am thankful to the Chairman of Lak Pohora and the Ministry officials, the Secretary and the Additional Secretaries who basically got involved and expedited this matter. But, what I would like to point out is that all these fertilizer that is required for a district is not required at one given time. It is over a period of time that these fertilizers are used. If the officials at the grass-roots level can work with the farmer organizations and work out a timetable in regard to the quantum of urea required when they start cultivation, how much of MOP is required and when, how much of TSP is required and when and if the transportation and the storage of these fertilizers are planned in such a manner, then it becomes difficult for those corrupt people to steal because when you have a thousand and you pull a hundred, it is not noticed because it is a hundred out of a thousand. But, if it is a hundred, you cannot pull a ten because it will be noticed. So what happens is, these officers thrive on getting everything in bulk. They will scream at the last minute and say, No urea, no urea. The total amount of urea is pushed into the district and you will find lorries and lorries leaving the district with urea. Why? They have been able to siphon out these extra quantities without a hum from the farmer because when the farmer is getting 1,000 kilograms or if he gets 575 kilograms, he does not mind the 25 kilograms which is half a bag not coming to him because he has got a big amount. He is taking it. He is leaving that 25 kilograms. If these 25 kilograms is multiplied by the number of farmers, you can see how many thousands of kilograms finally end up with these officers. This has to be better managed because we are subsidizing it. If a farmer has to buy urea at Rs. 1,000 a bag, he is going to buy only what he wants or a little less. But, when he has to only pay Rs. 350, he will buy what he wants and if possible, a little more. Why? Because he is getting it free. His Rs. 1,000 gives him much more than he could have otherwise bought. So, this needs to be co-ordinated and programmed so that the corrupt officials who are involved in this system are not given an opportunity to steal. Then we come to the other issue, which is organic fertilizer. I have read in certain reports that the organic content of the soil in Sri Lanka is very low and it could also be due to the high temperatures that are prevalent in our areas. Now, there was a lot of encouragement on the farmers to use organic fertilizer. But, this cannot be done by propaganda alone. There is no point in just having propaganda saying, okay, there are so much of incentives and this is how it will work. Again, this has to be coordinated. The farmer organizations, the Department of Agriculture, the Agrarian Services Division, have to get together and educate the farmer that if he does not reduce his dependence on inorganic fertilizer, he is bringing harm upon himself and the generations to follow. In fact, last week I was in my village and we have this system of having a kandoorie which means, a thanksgiving meal before we start harvesting. This is an

old tradition that is practised in that area and I had to attend three kandoories where we have all the farmers of that area getting together, and eating together and thanking God for having seen us through a season before we start harvesting. I used that session to educate these farmers as to the danger of the high potency agro chemicals that we continue to use. And, somebody innocently told me Sir, you are right, because that tract, that five acres they are spraying insecticide now, three days later they are going to harvest. So, we know that all the paddy that comes out of that tract is full of insecticides because according to the norms, you have to leave a crop standing for 14 days after application of insecticide for the residual effect to go out. Now, all this has to be taught by these officers. They have to be involved in the cultivation and they have to become a part of this farming process otherwise we will find that we are a nation who will be earning to pay the hospitals, because we see so many freak diseases, so much of things harming our citizens maybe because we are consuming rice that has a high level of insecticidal residue. Coming to again the subsidy that is managed, it is two weeks since harvesting started in the Ampara District and we have a circular which says that any farmer who is to qualify for the subsidy next season has to sell to the Government at the guaranteed price of Rs. 30, 500 kilograms per hectare that he cultivates. Now, two weeks have gone but the Government is not ready to purchase the paddy. The Treasury has not allocated funds, the Paddy Marketing Board has not opened its stores, they have not given the bags to the Agrarian Services Centres for distribution to the farmers for collection of paddy and what is happening? The private sector is buying the paddy at Rs. 28 a kilogram when the guaranteed price is Rs. 30. Now if this delays, if another two weeks passes, you will find that the harvesting is over. What the Government must realize is now the harvesting is mechanized in the country. Normally, when we spend four to five days to harvest a tract of paddy, now it is done within a day. A machine covers almost eight acres a day. So within two weeks all the harvesting is over; the people have taken the paddy home; they are in debt; they have to pay their debts, they have to sell the paddy at whatever price they can and when the Government opens its stores, the farmer does not have paddy. So, sometimes you wonder what is happening. If the Government is keen to build a buffer stock, the Government should be prepared long before harvest time to have the Paddy Marketing Board ready to buy the paddy. But, here two weeks have gone and I am sure another two weeks will go. Then, all the farmers will say, We have sold our paddy. We do not have paddy to sell. Then, the pressure comes on to the politician to ensure that the farmer gets a subsidy next season for no fault of his. So, I urge the Government that if it is really serious on building a buffer stock and buying paddy from the farmers, the Government should immediately release funds to the Government Agents, through whom I believe this scheme is operated, and the Paddy Marketing Boards
