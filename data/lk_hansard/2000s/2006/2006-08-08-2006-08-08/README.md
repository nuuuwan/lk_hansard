S.C.(S.D) No. 10/2006SRI LANKA ELECTRICITY BILL

S.C.(S.D) No. 11/2006THE CEYLON ELECTRICITYBOARD ACT, No. 17 OF 1969(Amendment) BILL

COUNSEL :Milinda Gunatilake, Senior StateCounsel for Attorney General assistedCourt.

Court assembled for hearing on 26th July 2006 at 10.30 a.m.

Written Submissions tendered on 27th July 2006.

The above-mentioned Bills have been referred to this Court byHis Excellency the President, in terms of Article 122(1)(b) of theConstitution for a special determination as to whether any Bill orany provision thereof is inconsistent with the Constitution. The Billsbear an endorsement of the Secretary to the Cabinet of Ministersthat in the view of the Cabinet of Ministers they are urgent in thenational interest.

The bills contain extensive provisions with regard to the generation,transmission and supply of electricity and the regulation thereof.Considering the importance of an efficient, co-ordinated andeconomical supply of electricity from the perspectives of the day today lives of the People on the one hand and industry and commercevital to the economy of the country on the other, a careful scrutinyof the provisions is necessary for a proper determination as toconstitutionality. Such a task is made more arduous by the limitedtime permitted in the Constitution for urgent Bills and the absenceof submissions of parties that will be affected. In the circumstanceswe have taken the bills as a whole and addressed and salient aspectsrelevant to make the determination that is required.

The generation, transmission, distribution, supply and use ofelectricity has been regulated by the Electricity Act No. 19 of 1950,as amended.

The basis of the regulatory framework contained in this Act is thatelectricity could be supplied only under a licence granted by theMinister. The Act contains provisions for licensing of the supply ofelectricity and, the construction and maintenance of works for suchpurpose. The generation and transmission of electricity was carriedout as a governmental function by the Department of GovernmentElectrical Undertakings (DGEU). The necessary implication of Section61 of the Act was the supply to consumers being done by localauthorities.

The Ceylon Electricity Board Act No. 17 of 1969 established astatutory body by that name (CEB) and in terms of Section 18, theDGEU, its movable and immovable property, rights and obligationswere transferred to the CEB. By an amendment to Section 2(1) ofthe Electricity Act the requirement of licensing was made inapplicablein respect of the supply of electricity by the CEB.

Sections 20 to 30 of the Electricity Board Act provided for thevoluntary or compulsory transfer of electrical undertaking of local

authorities for the supply of electricity within their areas, to the CEB. Interms of Section 19 no licence could be granted under the ElectricityAct, except in consultation with the CEB. Thus the CEB virtuallybecame the sole authority for generation, transmission and supply ofelectricity. Under this legal regime there has been a large increase ingeneration capacity both from hydro and thermal sources. The CEBhas also entered into power purchase agreements to meet the growingdemand. The expansion of the national grid, supply of electricity toindustries, enterprises, urban centres and rural electrification have beencarried out by the Board. It is in this context that the two bills referredto above that make extensive amendments have to be considered.

Electricity Reforms Act No. 28 of 2002 was certified on 11.12.2002,which provided for the repeal of the Electricity Act and the CeylonElectricity Board Act. Certain parts of this Act have been brought intooperation by an Order in gazette 1283/1 of 4.7.2003. A scheme forreorganization for the electricity industry was published in GazetteNo. 1321/21 dated 2.1.2004. But, the 9 Companies envisaged to beincorporated in terms of the scheme (Company X; Company Z; Disco1 to 5, GENCO and TRANSCO) were not in fact incorporated andtherefore the pre-existing legal regime has continued.

Senior State Counsel submitted that the two Bills referred to proceedon the basis of repealing Act No. 28 of 2002, which never becomeeffective.

The regulatory framework in the Electricity Act and Act No. 28 of2002 and the present Electricity Bill is broadly similar with thesignificant difference that the power of licensing vested in the Ministerunder the first Act passes to the Public Utilities Commission (PUC)established by Act No. 35 of 2002. The main aspects regarding thegeneration and the supply of electricity are dealt with in the Bill toamend the Ceylon Electricity Board Act. Hence the issues ofconstitutionality have to be dealt with from the perspective of this Bill.

The purpose of the Bill as evidenced in the amendment to the longtitle to Act No. 17 of 1969 is to provide for the generation,transmission, distribution and supply of electricity now vested in theC.E.B to be carried out through Electricity Utility Companies (EUCs)to be incorporated for that purpose in terms of the Bill.

Part 1A introduced by clause 7 requires the Board of the CEB, withinone month of the date of coming into force of this Part to prepare ascheme for the re-organization of the powers, duties and functions ofthe Board, in consultation with the Public Utilities Commission (PUC)(Section 13A (1).)

The object of the scheme thus prepared is as stated in section 13A(3)and (4) to identify the functions, presently discharged by the Boardand notionally separate them into three categories. They are :

i)electricity generation;

ii)transmission (and bulk power procurement)

iii)distribution and supply

The scheme should propose to the Minister the functions to be takenover by the Electricity Utility Companies (EUCs), one each forgeneration and transmission and two or more for distribution andsupply.

In terms of Section 13B, the Minister is required to submit the schemeprepared by the Board to Cabinet of Ministers and upon approval, inconsultation with the Minister of Finance to direct Registrar ofCompanies to incorporate Companies in terms of the Conversion ofPublic Corporations or Government Owned Business Undertakingsinto Public Companies Act No. 23 of 1987.

In regard to constitutionality we have examined certain aspects thatemerge from these provisions. These aspects are :

i)The properties of the C.E.B and its assets from the perspective ofits duties and transference thereof to the proposed EUCs;

ii)Pending litigation of the C.E.B and its obligations;

2006 0856

iii)The capital of EUCs, required for their functions; payment forprocurements of EUCs;

iv)The employees of the CEB and their continuance in service orretirement :

(1) The properties of the C.E.B and its assets from theperspective of its duties and transference thereof to theproposed E.U.Cs;

The transfer of properties and assets that are extensively spreadthroughout the country has to be viewed from the perspective of theirbeing owned, possessed and managed as an integrated single unit fromthe time of vesting from the DGEU and the continued acquisition overthe past 40 years. The distribution in terms of the Bill has to be based onfunctions and not on the basis of severable units or installations. Sucha process of breaking up of a well entrenched single proprietory andfunctional entity into distinct units, to be vested in different companieshaving separate legal personality, would raise a range of legal issues.However, there is only a single provision contained in clause 13A (4)(d) of the Bill with regard to this matter. This provision requires for thescheme to;

identify property both movable and immovable belonging to theBoard, that shall be required to be vested in any one or more of thecompanies proposed to be incorporated which would enable suchcompany or companies to discharge its functions efficiently andeffectively

Some property may be vested in more than one Company.

Senior State Counsel was unable to point out to any other provisioncontaining further guidelines as to the exercise of discretion on thisregard. As noted above all the installations of the C.E.B have beenmanaged hitherto as one entity e.g : the Kelanitissa Power Station builtand developed as one entity is the focal point of the national grid andis the place of generation of a bulk of thermal energy. Thus generation,transmission and distribution are all unified in this installation. Themanner of separation of this unified entity which has functionedefficiently for several decades would be in itself a electrifyingexperience.

Senior State Counsel submitted that the separation could be done inthe scheme to be prepared. The vesting of a discretion withoutadequate guidelines in the law is inconsistent with Article 12(1) of theConstitution, since it could result in an abuse of power thereby denyingto the People the equal protection of law.

Counsel relied on the scheme published in the Gazette in terms of ActNo. 28 of 2002 and cited two examples. They are :

As a general principle the boundary between Genco and Transcoshall be at high voltage bushings of the generator transformer(page 8A)

As a general principle the boundary between Transco and Discosshall be at the distribution line isolator of the grid substation(page 15A)

We have to note that they are only general principles. They cannotbe the basis of separation establishing distinct ownership of propertyand the creation of rights and liabilities. More importantly, there wouldbe a dereliction of the clearly identified responsibility now vested inlaw on the CEB by Section 11(1) of the Ceylon Electricity Board ActNo. 17 of 1969 which reads as follows :

It shall be duty of the Board, with effect from the date of transferto the Board of the Government Electrical Undertakings underSection 18, to develop and maintain an efficient, co-ordinatedand economical system of electricity supply for the whole of SriLanka.

This is the foremost duty of the CEB in respect of which severalancillary duties are specified in Section 11(2), (a) to (g).

Sub-paragraph (g) reads as follows :

to develop a sound, adequate and uniform electricity policy,and, for that purpose, to control and utilize national powerresources

These provisions in Section 11 are in our view an essential legalsafeguard to ensure an efficient co-ordinated and economic Servicethat is vital to the life of the community. A distruption of a vital servicehas a serious impact on the People as evidenced in the distruptions ofPetroleum Supplies and port Services. A disruption of electricity wouldhave far more serious consequences. Hence if at all the vital duty ascontained in section 11 should, be strengthened. But, on the contrary,the amending Bill by clause 5 repeals Section 11. The responsibility isfragmented into imprecise units and only a semblance of Section 11 iscontained in the proposed section 13A which reads as follows :

(d) identify property both movable and immovable belonging tothe Board, that shall be required to be vested in any one or more of thecompanies proposed to be incorporated, which would enable suchcompany or companies to discharge its functions efficiently and effec-tively;

This would be in our view a denial of the protection of law assuredto the People by section 11 and be inconsistent with Article 12(1) of theConstitution.

The next aspect to be considered in this regard is the manner inwhich the property and assests of the Ceylon Electricity Board wouldbecome vested in the respective EUCs. A sufficient legal regims isrequired in this aspect to assure to the People an efficient and effectivesupply of electricity.

Senior State Counsel submitted that the vesting would take place interms of the provisions of the Conversion of public Corporation orGovernment Owned Business Undertakings into Public Companies ActNo. 23 of 1987. The provision of the Bill that links up to Act No. 23 of1987 is contained clause 13C(1), which provides for the Minister todirect the Registrar of Companies to incorporate the EUCs in terms ofsection 2(1) of Act No. 23 of 1987 to take over the three functions ofthe Board relating to generation, transmission distribution and supply.

There is no provision for the Minister to submit the scheme to theRegistrar for the purpose of separating the assets and properties of theCEB into three or more EUCs that are to be incorporated. Indeed, theRegistrar has no capacity or competency to engage in the exercise ofseparating the properties and installations of the CEB located through-out the country to the EUCs that are to be incorporated.

In the circumstances we would be left with the provisions of Section3(1) of Act No. 23 of 1987 which merely states that upon publicationof the incorporation order in the Gazette that all property movable andimmovable, rights, powers and privileges, books of accounts of theCorporation shall be vested in the company, referred to in the order. Ifthe entirety of the functions of the C.E.B are vested in one Company,the provision of section 3(1) would be adequate. However, in terms ofthe Bill the position is quite to the contrary. The C.E.B itself will con-tinue as a legal entity and there will be three or more other EUCs. In thecircumstances there would be considerable uncertainty with regard tothe particular assets and properties that would remain with the C.E.B orvested in the three or more EUCs to be established. This uncertaintywould result in the EUCs being unable to function efficiently andeffectively so as to supply electricity to the people who would bethereby denied the equal protection of the law guaranteed in terms ofArticle 12(1) of the Constitution.

(2) Pending litigation of the CEB and its obligations.

There is no provision in the Bill with regard to transfer of pendinglitigation, contracts and outstanding liabilities of the CEB.7 PL 001375 - (2006/08)

78

Senior State Counsel submitted that the entirety of the proposal forrestructuring has been conceived in view of the current indebtedness ofthe CEB and its failure to levy a market rate for the electricity suppliedto the consumers. If so a serious question arises as to the manner inwhich these liabilities are to be retained or transferred to the EUCs.There is no provision whatsoever in the Bill in respect of this vitalaspect which seriously impacts on the rights of the creditors and thirdparties who have entered into transactions with the CEB. A similarproblem would arise with regard to pending litigation. The resultingposition is in our view is an inconsistency with Article 12(1) of theConstitution.

(3) The capital of EUCs, required for their functions ; paymentsfor procurements of EUCs.

Although EUCs would be incorporated in terms of clause 13C andsection 2(1) of Act No. 23 of 1987 as noted above, there is no provisionwith regard to the capital of an EUC. The only provision is contained inSection 2(3) of Act No. 23 of 1987 which states that upon publicationof the order in the Gazette, the Registrar of Companies shall allot all theshares into which share capital of the company is divided to the secre-tary to the Treasury (in his official capacity) for and on behalf of theState.

Hence the EUCs would not have a liquid share capital and be devoidof resources to carry out its functions.

Senior State Counsel submitted that the assets of the CEB would bethe capital of an EUC. This argument merely begs the question since asnoted above there is no provision in the scheme for the separation ofeach asset. In any event capital here should be liquid assets and if theCEB is heavily indebted as claimed there will certainly be none fortrasfer to EUCs. There may be no possibility of EUCs raising capitalby a share issue, since a minimum of 50% of shares and the control ofeach EUCs would remain with the CEB. The absence of any capital forthe EUC to discharge its vital functions of generation, transmission andsupply of electricity to consumers would deny people of the equalprotection of the law guaranteed to them by Article 12(1) of the Con-stitution.

As envisaged in the law a Generating Company has to meet itsprocurement payment and recover from the Transmission Companythat will recover from the Distribution Company who will recoverfrom Consumers. This is a fragile procurement payment system whichwould impact on the financial strength of EUCs.

(4) The employees of the C.E.B and their continuance in service orretirement :

Section 13D of the Bill deals with the officers and servants of theCEB. The re - organisation scheme provides for an option being of-fered to present employees as follows :

a) to accept employment in EUC which is offered ;

b) to continue in service as an employee of the CEB;

c) to accept neither and be paid compensation in terms of theVoluntary Retirement Scheme (VRS).

We note that employment in an EUC is only dependant on suchemployment being offered by the CEB. The Section does not containany guidelines on the basis of which such offer would be made. Theabsence of an assurance of employment being offered in the same postthat an officer is working or of guidelines as to the exercise of discre-tion when making an offer of employment in a EUC, results in ourview an inconsistency and Article 12(1) of the Constitution.

For the reasons stated above we make a determination that the provi-sions of the Ceylon Electricity Board Amendment Act referred to aboveand the Ceylon Electricity Bill that necessarily gets interlinked with theprovisions of the former Bill, taken as a whole are inconsistent withArticle 12(1) of the Constitution and require to be passed with a specialmajority as specified in Article 84(2) of the Constitution.

( [ )(The Hon. Rauff Hakeem)

Bismillahir Rahmanir Raheem

Mr. Speaker, the Sri Lanka Muslim Congress, as a partythat has received a preponderant mandate repeatedly fromthe Muslims, is duty-bound to bring to the attention of thisaugust Assembly and place on record the grave injusticecaused to the innocent civilian population of Mutur, Toppurand other areas and the surrounding villages as a result of therecent military engagement between the LTTE and theGovernment of Sri Lanka that was aimed at gaining controlof territory.

The Government of Sri Lanka whilst claiming that theiroffensive was to ensure the security of the State, and theLTTE the areas under their control have continued with

absolute impunity to desecrate the security of the innocentcivilian population caught up in the cross-fire. Ever since theinvasion of Mutur by the LTTE, my appeals and state-ments have, unfortunately, been the subject of critical retortby some elements in the Government. In the meantime, insome print media and websites supportive of the LTTE in-cluding the Tamil Net, my statements have been taken out ofcontext and portions of my statements have been used togive the impression that I have attributed disproportionateblame on the Government forces.

All of us are aware that in war the first casualty is truth.Thousands of unfortunate victims of the Mutur eviction, whoare languishing in very deplorable conditions, arerecounting their harrowing experiences at the hands of theLTTE and the deliberate, unceasing artillery and mortarattacks from security forces camps with the objective offlushing out the LTTE despite calls to suspend this mode ofattack. Unfortunately, this has resulted in scores of peopledying and the constructive expulsion of Muslims caused bythe LTTEs surprise attack.

The LTTE claims that they had withdrawn their forces topre-CFA positions after having achieved the limitedobjective of attacking selected targets of the Sri Lankan forces.Today 50,000 people from areas in and around Mutur havefled to Kantalai, Tampalakamam, Kinniya and more refugeesare now pouring in from there to many areas in Kurunegala,Anuradhapura and Polonnaruwa Districts. Colombo andKandy Districts too have begun to receive small numbers ofthese unfortunate victims of war.

Vivid details of the atrocious and inhuman treatment metedout to the fleeing civilian population by the LTTE cadres inthe vicinity of Pachchanoor on the Mutur-Kantalai Road,starkly exposes the LTTEs brutality. The thousands ofcivilians who left Mutur on an assurance by the LTTE that noharm would be caused to them if they chose to leave, weresubjected to such harassment and cruelty resulting in theMuslims realizing that, as in the instances before, the LTTEsassurances could not be taken for granted.

From the morning of 4th August, thousands of fleeingcivilians including children, pregnant mothers and theelderly, who have been subjected to virtual starvation forover 48 hours as a result of incessant attacks on Mutur bothby the Government forces and the LTTE, were forciblydiverted to Kiranthimunai off the 64th Milepost on theMutur-Kantalai Road. There, the armed cadres of the LTTEseparated young and able-bodied men from women, childrenand the elderly. Some of the cadres were seen beating thewomen, the children and the elderly, who chose to plead onbehalf of the detained men. An artillery attack which targetedthis checkpoint, according to scores of eyewitnesses, resultedin the death of some civilians as well as a few LTTE cadres. Inthe ensuing confusion, many civilians managed to escapefrom the LTTE cadres and reach Kantalai after trekking formany hours through inhospitable terrain.

[ ]I am informed, Mr. Speaker, that right now there are manybodies, over 60, found by the security forces, lying in theKiranthimunai area. Most of them are Muslims. By the lookof the bodies, some of them, according to witnesses whohave seen from a distance through telescopes with the helpof the armed forces, appear to have their hands tied and shot.This is a serious violation of human rights and would amountto a war crime if the fact is established. We have tried to getthese bodies released through the ICRC. But, unfortunately,the international Committe of the Red Cross has not found itpossible to reach this area.

Quite paradoxically, the Government stated that they areresorting to a limited offensive operation on humanitariangrounds to help the farmers who were served by the MavilAru anicut. Similarly, the LTTE, in its statement announcingthe withdrawal of their forces to pre - CFA positions all overthe Trincomalee District, also stated that they are doing so inconsideration of the humanitarian crisis. On Sunday the 06th,we heard of the offer by the LTTE to voluntarily open thesluice gates of the Mavil Aru anicut provided the Governmentagrees to certain conditions.

All peace - loving people of Sri Lanka have come to realizethat both the Government and the LTTE continue to makesanctimonious pronouncements supposedly with theobjective of respecting the humanitarian needs of the people.In real terms, their motive is to pursue a relentless militaryoption, totally disregarding the sanctity of life and limb ofinnocent civilians. The Muslim, the Tamil and the Sinhalapeople of this area have suffered in innumerable ways. Thepsychological trauma experienced by these people is goingto leave an indelible impact on their psyche for years tocome. The cost of the ammunition, the bombs and the militaryhardware that are employed and destroyed by both sides tothis conflict could have fed the affected farmers of Mavil Aruand the other victims for many more months.

In terms of the Ceasefire Agreement, it is the Governmentof Sri Lanka that is responsible for human security, particularlyin Government - controlled areas. The events that unfoldedin the recent past reiterate the SLMCs repeated call that theCFA must be reviewed and strengthened to address thelegitimate concerns of all stakeholders and every possiblestep should be taken to protect and ensure the security of thecivilian population at all times. It is also our considered viewthat these incidents cannot be seen in isolation, but need tobe viewed together with the events of the recent past,including the notices that were distributed in Mutur, callingfor the Muslims to vacate the areas. It is also pertinent toanalyze as to whether the Government of Sri Lanka hasunwittingly been lured into a trap, where it could easily beaccused of triggering the exodus of the people of Mutur orhave the redical elements clamouring for war as a solution tothe ethnic problem skillfully achieved their objective ofpitting the Muslims against the Tamils.

It is also our reiterated position that, as a responsiblepolitical party, we are committed to finding a lasting, durableand dignified peace that would be acceptable to allcommunities through negotiations. It is in this backdrop thatwe find it difficult to endorse the indiscriminate violationsof the Ceasefire Agreement that have resulted in the forcedeviction of Muslims from areas of their traditional habitationand systematic destruction of the political and economicpower of the Muslims of the Northern and the EsternProvinces.

Thank you, Mr. Speaker.
