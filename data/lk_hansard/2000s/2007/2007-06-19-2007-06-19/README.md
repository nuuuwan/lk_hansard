Deputy Speaker, I think it is appropriate on this occasion to provide this august Assembly with some information relating to the highlights of the work which is currently being done by the Ministry of Export Development and International Trade. play a vital role in invigorating Sri Lankas economy. Our approach is that we have to make the maximum use of the bilateral agreements which are now in existence by working towards entering into bilateral agreements with other countries particularly in the SAARC region. We also propose to derive the maximum advantage from multilateral treaties which exist at present and it is our conviction that the interest of this country is best served by a combination of these two approaches, multilateral and bilateral. They are by no means mutually exclusive. If I might give you an example, Sir, we have been able to gain very considerable advantages from negotiating with the Government of Pakistan pointing out certain anomalies and deficiencies that are inherent in the Free Trade Agreement which exist between the Governments of Sri Lanka and Pakistan. I made a personal appeal to the Hon. Minister of Trade and Commerce of Pakistan, Hon. Hamayun Aktar Khan to give us certain concessions particularly in the agricultural sector and I am happy to tell the House that the response by the Government of Pakistan was entirely favourable. In consequence of discussions that took place in Islamabad we were able to obtain for the farmers of this country very far-reaching concessions especially in respect of the betel industry. According to the Free Trade Agreement that had been concluded there was an upper threshold of 12,000 metric tons in respect of betel and this is the limit that was withdrawn as a result of the representations that we made and since then, we have visited the districts which are most affected by this namely, Kegalle, Kurunegala and Gampaha and we are now together with the representatives of the farmers organizations evolving a procedure that would enable us to make the maximum use of these concessions which have been placed at our disposal by the Government of Pakistan. In a similar spirit, Mr. Deputy Speaker, we have engaged in intensive negotiations with the Government of India. We have been able to persuade the Government of India to remove various non-tariff barriers which have impeded the progress that is possible within the framework of the FTA. For example, one of these non-tariff barriers was that for the export of tea out of Sri Lanka into India, we were not able to use the port of Chennai. We had to use either Cochin or Calcutta and that was a very serious disadvantage. But, the Government of India has now accepted our representations in this regard and some of these obstacles have been very rapidly removed. As you are aware, Mr. Deputy Speaker, we are now extending the Free Trade Agreement. We have started discussions with regard to a comprehensive economic partnership agreement. That is, we are moving from physical commodities into services. The Sri Lankan industry can benefit enormously from the expansion of the contours within which they can operate. Already the construction sector has accepted some very lucrative contracts in the Maldives, in India, in the Gulf area and it is the duty of our Ministry to strengthen these initiatives in every possible way.

The GSP-Plus arrangement with the European Union are of fundamental importance for a whole variety of industries in this country. About a month ago we had some very productive discussions in Brussels. I had the occasion to talk to Mr. Peter Mandelson, the Trade Commissioner of the European Union and the outlook is bright we think not only to retain the advantages that we enjoy at present but also to expand some of these concessions so that the industries other than the apparel industry can also benefit from privileged entry into the markets of the European continent. While all this is taking place we are working on the SAFTA Agreement examining how Sri Lanka can benefit to the maximum possible extent from these arrangements and cumulatively the arrangements that we are putting in place will be a source of very considerable strength to the Sri Lankan economy at the present time. So that is the first aspect of the matter, Mr. Deputy Speaker. The second aspect has to do with the work that is being done by the Export Development Board which functions under my Ministry. The Export Development Board consists of 20 Sectoral Committees comprising representatives of the respective industries together with officials of the Department and the Ministry. In every sector there are very considerable advances which can take place and I am certain that within the next year or so some of these will become a reality. Let us take for example the fruit and vegetable sector. Enormous advances are still possible in this sector. We have spoken to the principal people representing the industry. They have told us that they can increase very considerably the volume of exports out of this country. But there are certain facilities and services which they need. In particular, they want a land to be given to them; not to buy the land. They are not interested in that at all but on short-term leases to cultivate the land. We have already identified the lands that can be made available for this purpose. The export clusters which function under the Ministry of Finance and Planning are working with my Ministry. We know what the lands that are available in different parts of the country are. We know the requirements of people who are engaged in the cultivation of vegetables and fruits for export into Maldives, into Singapore, into Dubai, into Qatar and so on. This is a very promising area for further development. The gem and jewellery industry is another sector. Together with my distinguished Colleague, the Hon. (Dr.) Sarath Amunugama, we are trying to make credit available to the gem and jewellery industry. One of the problems here, Mr. Deputy Speaker, is that capital is difficult for them to obtain. If you are prepared to mortgage a piece of land as collateral, you will have no difficulty in obtaining a loan from the bank because the bank has objective criteria by reference to which they can assess the value of the land and they can then determine how much money they can make available to the industrialist. In the case of gems, it is much more difficult.

[ . ]

About a month ago I sent for representatives of all the commercial banks in this country and some of them have agreed to set up a fund, seed capital, that would enable the gem and jewellery industry to be developed in this country and that would be particularly important for a district like Ratnapura represented by the Hon. John Senewiratne. So, this is some of the on-going work that is being done in these different areas. It is my firm conviction, Mr. Deputy Speaker, that the way forward lies through value addition. We can no longer export raw material out of this country. There has to be value addition into tea, into rubber, into cinnamon and that is the way to provide employment for young people. Recently, we gave some awards to the principal exporters of Sri Lanka. The largest exporter was a company called LodeStar which has a dominant position not just in the regional market but in the world market as well. What they do is they make use of high quality latex and they manufacture pneumatic rubber tyres. They have a very considerable share of the world market. As far as tea is concerned instead of simply exporting tea, companies like Delma and Mlesna resort to value addition on a significant scale and that has resulted in a considerable increase in the volume of foreign exchange that we earn for the country. There is one point that I would like to make in that connection with emphasis. Our aim is not simply to increase the amount of money that we earn. That is important but that is not enough. It is crucial to ensure that there is a flow down of these economic benefits to the grass roots in order to achieve an uplifment of economic conditions in different parts of the country. I have visited several parts of the country such as Anuradhapura, Polonnaruwa and Uva Wellassa. I also visited Welimada, Kurunegala, Gampaha, all these regions and I have seen for myself how much it is possible to improve the economy of these areas by developing exports in an appropriate manner. So while we are developing exports in order to enhance the total volume of foreign exchange that we earn for our country, we want to ensure that the profits will reach the farmers. This is very important and I am very happy to tell you, Mr. Deputy Speaker, that in the first three months of 2007 exports have grown by 13 per cent approximately. When you compare the figures for the first three months of 2007 with the corresponding period for 2006 there has been a very considerable expansion, approximately 13 per cent increase in the value of exports out of Sri Lanka and that is one of the strong features of the Sri Lankan economy at the present time. I will not take much of your time, Mr. Deputy Speaker, but make some very brief remarks about the third aspect which has to do with the another institution that functions under my Ministry namely, the Sri Lanka Export Credit Insurance Corporation. That has a very vital role to play

particularly with regard to people who are leaving the country in search of employment. It is particularly important for women who are going in search of employment. It is the function of the Sri Lanka Export Credit Insurance Corporation to give loans and other facilities to enable these people to settle down comfortably in a new environment. The money is made available by five commercial banks but on the basis of a guarantee that is given by the Sri Lanka Export Credit Insurance Corporation. This is known as the APARA Scheme and as an instrument of social equity, I think it is a very timely and appropriate initiative. So, this is some of the work that is being done by our Ministry at the present time, Mr. Deputy Speaker. I appreciate your indulgence in allowing me to place before the House some of the salient facts relating to the important programmes which have been undertaken by the Ministry of Export Development and International Trade. I thank you, Sir.
