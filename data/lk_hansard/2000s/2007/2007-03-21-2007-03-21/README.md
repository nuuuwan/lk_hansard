) (The Hon. A. H. M. Fowzie - Minister of Petroleum and Petroleum Resources Development) Mr. Deputy Speaker, there are three pieces of legislation being discussed in this House today. Although they are different in nature, I believe it is very important to implement them effectively if we are to ensure protection to our environment and natural resources in the name of future generations. As far as I remember since early 2000, the Ministry of Environment has embarked on a number of programmes to reduce the environmental impacts of polythene. I am happy to note that some initiatives that were taken during my tenure as the Minister of Environment and Natural Resources are now being implemented through Gazette Extraordinary No. 1466/5 dated 10th October, 2006.

We need to recognize that the main problem with the polythene products is related to its improper disposal. Developed countries have more than 20 times per capita use of polythene and plastic than ours. However, as these countries have very good disposal systems, there is very little impact on the environment. In Sri Lanka we know that discarded polythene, especially the shopping bags cause immense environmental and health hazards. The flash-floods in urban areas and even the dengue epidemic are direct results of haphazard disposal of polythene products. Polythene is a product that can be used and reused over and over again by recycling. However, reuse and recycling of very thin polythene products such as Sili Sili bags is practically difficult. Therefore, while we promote reuse of polythene products through recycling, we also have to bring in some control in the use of thin products. Therefore, the regulations that are being proposed to regulate the use of thin polythene with thickness of 20 microns and below is very timely. As this Regulation has come into effect from January this year, we can see a very positive response from the general public. Now there are people very eagerly looking for our traditional food wrappers like the banana leaves in place of the thin lunch sheets which is sold in millions in number. Sir, while we control the use of thin polythene, we also must have a programme to encourage recycling and reuse. In 2004, I proposed levying a cess of 0.5 per cent on import of all plastic raw materials. The funds generated from the cess were proposed to be used to promote recycling of polythene wastes and also to introduce biodegradable polythene products. I am happy to note that Rs. 50 million out of the cess proceeds has been given to the Ministry of Environment to launch this recycling programme starting from this year. Concurrently we must promote alternative products and also biodegradable polythene. As far as I am aware, there are no Sri Lanka standards for biodegradable polythene. Therefore, immediate action should be taken to develop Sri Lanka standards for biodegradable polythene and promote local production. Another important aspect is to provide incentives to promote alternative products. We have our traditional products like the Pan Malla. These products can be promoted through the Laksala and also through village-based production programmes. I would like to propose that an incentive programme be introduced to promote alternative products. Mr. Deputy Speaker, now I would like to talk about the problems involved with sand mining in river beds and on land. During the recent years, the high demand on sand for the construction industry has created heavy

2007 21 2049 2050

pressure on sand resources. This situation has led to many illegal activities such as unauthorized mining and illegal transportation. This kind of situation has resulted in the introduction of new regulations to control those activities. One such instance is the Regulations published on 17th July, 2006 banning the mechanized mining of sand in river beds. In addition, mechanized sand mining in rivers has also been prohibited by the National Sand Policy. While we bring in regulations to control sand mining on land we must promote the use of an alternative such as sea sand. The use of sea sand for construction work has been proven successful in many other countries. There is very limited usage of sea sand in Sri Lanka. Therefore, the Government has to find ways to promote the use of sea sand. The use of sea sand also has its limitations. It is necessary to keep the sea sand exposed to natural weather conditions to allow the washing out of salt. Sea sand is comparatively cheaper. I would like to propose that the Government agencies should be involved in large-scale sea sand mining. There are Government agencies which have sea sand mining technologies. The Government must concentrate in supplying sand for large construction works. Mr. Deputy Speaker, the next important thing we are discussing today is the declaration of Sinharaja Forest as a National Wilderness Heritage Area. As we all are aware, the Sinharaja Forest is the largest single block of forest in the wet zone of Sri Lanka. This forest is more than 10,000 hectares in extent. It spreads over three districts namely, Ratnapura, Galle and Matara. Sinharaja is a very important forest in terms of its rich biodiversity and also for its hydrological values. Out of 840 flowering plants endemic in Sri Lanka, 495 can be seen in Sinharaja. Out of 340 timber tree species endemic to Sri Lanka, 192 can be seen in this forest. Forty species of mammals are found in Sinharaja and seven of them are endemic to Sri Lanka. There are 147 bird species in Sinharaja and 25 out of 26 endemic species are found in this forest. Therefore, the Sinharaja forest is biologically very unique and for the sake of conservation of global and national biological diversity we must take every possible step to conserve this important block of forest. Having considered its national importance and uniqueness, this forest has been declared as a National Heritage Wilderness Area; vide Gazette No. 528/14 dated 21st October, 1988. The UNESCO has subsequently declared this as a World Heritage Wilderness Area due to its global significance. Therefore, this is the only World Heritage Wilderness Area found in Sri Lanka. Therefore, we must take every

possible step to protect this national and world heritage. Despite these efforts there are still some threats to the existence of this unique forest area. There are a number of forest blocks in the buffer zone of Sinharaja which are either owned by private parties or the LRC. I am aware that over the last few decades or so, all the governments have made various attempts to include these adjoining forests into the Sinharaja but to-date none of them have been successful. Therefore, I propose to revive these efforts and take over such adjoining forests into Sinharaja. Apart from the Sinharaja, there are other forests such as Knuckles, Horton Plains and peak wilderness which have both cultural and natural values. Therefore, we must take early action to declare these forests as national and world heritage areas. The cultural value of these forests are equally important as much as their biological and ecological values. The present efforts of the Minister of Environment and Natural Resources to declare them as world heritage areas should be expedited.
