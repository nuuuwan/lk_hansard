Hon. Deputy Chairman, today, we are discussing the Votes of various institutions and Commissions listed under the Head of His Excellency the President. I must, at the very outset, say that His Excellency the President, having got a very resounding mandate, has completed one year in office and going by what he said when he addressed the nation two days ago, it appears that he is still under certain inhibitions about criticisms that have been levelled against him for his performance during the past one year. Of course, I believe that we must give him a little more time for him to bring all his programmes to fruition. It is one Presidential Election where the entire country votes as one electorate and selects the Head of State, who is now vested with unbridled Executive power further strengthened by the Twentieth Amendment. He had always said that he would be guided by expert advice and scientific advice on all matters. In that regard, he has set up a variety of committees.

81 82

Before coming to those issues, let me, first of all, deal with the Human Rights Commission of Sri Lanka, which came into being with bipartisan support by Parliament almost 20 years ago. In the history of the Human Rights Commission, we have seen ups and downs about its performance, but the issue where its recommendations lacking teeth, lacking powers of implementation has always been the primary issue of concern. The Human Rights Commission has the power to visit detention centres, prisons, police stations, interview detainees and submit recommendations. But, the institutions and office-bearers who have to carry out those recommendations do not respond to those recommendations. From the IGP downwards, from the Commissioner of Prisons downwards, this is a lacuna that has been observed. In the meantime, the Human Rights Commission also suffers from lack of funding and it is advisable to give them sufficient funding so that they do not go begging for funds from outside sources. Therefore, that is an issue regarding which the Government must pay more attention, to make it more vibrant and empower it to do its work. Particularly regarding school admissions, they give directions to Directors of Education. But, Directors of Education, as usual practice, have continuously been ignoring their recommendations. The only option available to the aggrieved party is to petition the Supreme Court. By the time the Human Rights Commission gives its finding, the time limit that is there to go before the Supreme Court has lapsed. So, those are the issues that had always been bothering the aggrieved parties and therefore, in regard to the Human Rights Commission, these issues have to be addressed.

Coming back to the various other issues concerning the powers of the President, I must say that there are some grievances about this COVID-19 programme and distribution of allowances to the people who have been suffering due to lockdown for a number of days now. Now, in various areas of Colombo, Valaichenai in Batticaloa and in several other areas, the Grama Niladharis are only giving the allowance for 100 families per day. There are some Grama Niladhari Divisions which have more than 2000 or 3000 families. So, if you go at that rate, all these people who have lost their livelihoods, who are unable to go out and engage in their usual vocations, would be deprived of their incomes and their survival is a big question. Therefore, the Divisional Secretariats must deploy more people. Some of the Grama Niladharis are now in mortal fear of contracting the disease. Some of the Grama Niladharis have already contracted the disease. Because of that, they are not even doing their normal duties. I know they are functioning under severe stress and difficulties. Therefore, they have to be supplemented with better staff and better support by the Divisional Secretariats so that these allowances could be distributed in time.

Sir, there are certain task forces that have been appointed under His Excellency the President. One Task Force which was referred to by the previous speaker relates to protection of archaeological and heritage sites in the Eastern Province. I simply cannot understand why there is a task force which only focuses on the Eastern

Province. There are several other archaeological and heritage sites all over the country. With 500 odd so-called sites which are of national importance on heritage and various other necessities in that province, maybe, the Government would have felt that some focus needs to be given to that area. But, in the meantime, there must be some mechanism that should be set up to engage with the civil society and the political authorities in that area so that there would be a discussion on matters of contention because these are all very emotional, sentimental issues where there are allegations without substantive evidence. Any allegation must be evidence-based, not because somebody is claiming that these lands belong to them. When it comes to archaeological sites, putting up of new buildings is not allowed. Archaeological Sites of National Importance Act very clearly states that it is to protect the artifacts and the monuments that are there. When it comes to archaeological sites, in putting up buildings, the legislation has always been observed in the breach. So, I would rather say that since there are these issues, the people of the area are always willing to cooperate, provided there is substantial discussion on these matters with the civil society as well as with the people who have got the mandate from the people of the area. I think there must be a proper mechanism set up so that these discussions can be started. Then, that Task Force also lacks diversity. It should have representation by all communities. That Task Force consisting of one community cannot be expected to do justice by everybody. That is seemingly a distortion in the formation of the Task Force itself.

Coming back to the composition of the various Commissions and independent institutions coming under the Presidency, it is important to respect the diversity in the country and appoint people from different communities as mandated by the Statute, and even where the Statute does not mandate, it is advisable that the diversity of the country be respected in making those appointments.

Then, I would say that the issue relating to gender must also be taken into account. Now, we have increased the number in the composition of the Election Commission from three to five since there was an issue about the quorum. I would prefer that the gender issue also be addressed and that women are also considered for appointment to some of these important Commissions as their contribution to this country, particularly when it comes to elective politics and representation in this country, has been an issue of contention for a long time. Therefore, it would be a good thing for the Government to consider appointing a woman to the Election Commission together with the rest of the Commissions. We had a lady, Dr. Deepika Udagama, leading the Human Rights Commission and she did a wonderful job. Unfortunately, she resigned. The Human Rights Commission has been functioning without a Chairman for several months ever since she resigned. Therefore, those issues have got to be resolved. I hope the Government will take these criticisms into account and redress those issues.
