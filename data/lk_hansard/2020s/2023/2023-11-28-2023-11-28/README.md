Hon. Prime Minister, the first is a matter that has generally been spoken of both by the Tamil and Muslim Members of Parliament. In fact, I asked the Hon. Yadamini Gunawardena whether I could meet you, Hon. Prime Minister, some time to discuss this matter. This is with regard to the Kalmunai North Divisional Secretariat in the Ampara District. Hon. Prime Minister, in 1989, Kalmunai North was recognized as a new AGA Division. Then, thereafter, in the seventh month of 1993, Cabinet approval was obtained for it to be made a Divisional Secretarys Division. Since then, it has been functioning as such, except for the accountant and the land office being transferred to that Division. So, the Kalmunai Divisional Secretariat, at the moment, holds those, but for all other purposes - if you look at the cadre also - Kalmunai North is recognized and is functioning as a fully-fledged Divisional Secretariat. Now, the problem, Hon. Prime Minister, is that there is a lot of political pressure that is being brought, which I am aware of, to downgrade that into a sub-office which, in fact, is not. One of the key tactics that is being used is the prevention of the appointment of an accountant and not transferring the land office from the current Kalmunai Divisional Secretariat to the Kalmunai North Divisional Secretariat. Therefore, Hon. Prime Minister, I humbly request you to intervene in this matter.

The Hon. Rauff Hakeem said that his party, in principle, is not opposed to recognizing the Kalmunai North Tamil Divisional Secretariat, but he said that it must be done after the boundaries are relooked at. This story of boundaries being relooked at has been going on for years and years or for decades. Surely, that is not a reason to prevent the appointment of an accountant and the land office to be moved to the Divisional Secretariat of Kalmunai North for it to be functioning fully. In fact, allowing it to function as a fully-fledged Divisional Secretariat will, then, give impetus for our Muslim Colleagues representing the Ampara District to move the matter with regard to redrawing some of these boundaries forward. We, in principle, agree that some of these boundaries have to be redrawn. But, that is not a reason to delay appointing an accountant and moving the land office to the Kalmunai North Divisional Secretariat.

Then, Hon. Prime Minister, vouchers have not been issued by the Kalmunai Divisional Secretariat for one-and-a-half months because the monies are kept in the Kalmunai Divisional Secretariat and there is a deliberate political interference. So, for one- and-a-half months, these people are waiting without vouchers being issued to them. Therefore, I think it is extremely unfair for this matter to be dragged on. You would recall that the President, when he was Prime Minister, agreed, in principle, to appoint an accountant and there is no reason whatsoever to delay that. With re-demarcation of boundaries, we will cooperate and make sure that it is done as soon as possible after the Kalmunai North Divisional Secretariat is fully functional.

The second point I wish to bring up is this. Hon. Prime Minister, there was a circular issued providing relief to the public officers in the local government service and in local government corporations, who were deprived of employment due to the situation that prevailed in the country after July, 1983. It was primarily to do with both the Sinhala Civil Service officers working in the North and the Tamil Civil Service officers working outside the North and the East, who were affected.

That particular circular has been extended repeatedly and I believe it is still relevant. The officers in the North and the East who got affected due to the war up to 2009 have sought relief under that, but, unfortunately, still, nothing is being processed. I think that is completely unfair, Hon. Prime Minister, because they are still not being given the due salary rises, their service for that period has not been counted and increments have been denied to them. Therefore, I ask you to intervene in this matter. I do not blame the officials in the Ministry, but at some administrative level, I believe, there is a complete lacklustre approach to these applications.

One last matter I would like to raise, Hon. Prime Minister, since you are the Minister in charge of public administration. As you know, for the last week ending on 27th November, we have been commemorating the "Great Heroes Week" and Hon. Prime Minister, despite our differences of opinion with regard to the LTTE and politics in general, if you look at those events, you would admit that there is nothing about LTTE in those, that simply, publicly, people had been coming together to mourn their dead and that there was no LTTE paraphernalia. In fact, the police, due to some Government instructions, had approached courts right throughout the North and the East against the commemorations and except for the Court in Sampur in Trincomalee, all other courts permitted the commemoration, provided that there is no overt LTTE paraphernalia involved and a promotion of the LTTE as such. These events had been taking place over the last several years and there had been no LTTE ideology or anything of that sort being shown. There are two issues related to this: either, to the cemeteries created on government lands, the Government is refusing to allow these people to go and commemorate their dead or the military, one of the three forces, occupying private lands used as cemeteries are refusing to vacate them. They are private lands also used as cemeteries, which the Government, through the military, has subsequently been occupying and they are refusing to vacate them.

Hon. Prime Minister, the Human Rights Commission of Sri Lanka recommended in 2015, during that era, that every single dead family member, even if they were connected to the LTTE, people have a right to remember. That is the law. We are talking about reconciliation; we are talking about having transitional justice and also about a Truth and Reconciliation Commission, which is part of the transitional justice process. Commemorating the dead is the fifth pillar of transitional justice. So, surely, to deny that, I think, is extremely unfair. Therefore, Hon. Prime Minister, you must intervene in this. This is a humanitarian matter. Those private lands

I do not think that is going to be a major issue for us but to prevent it, year in and year out, and to continue the traumatization of those people is just not on, Hon. Prime Minister and I ask you, as the Minister in charge of the Divisional Secretariates under which these lands come, particularly the government lands, to intervene in this matter and to give relief to those suffering parents and family members. ( ) (The Hon. Deputy Speaker) . [6.28 p.m.] ) ( - ) (The Hon. Keheliya Rambukwella - Minister of Environment) Hon. Deputy Speaker, I think we are now taking this issue in a different direction. I would like to tell the Hon. Member that we have not issued any mining licence.

This country has inherited so much of natural wealth and it is up to us to look into how best, while preserving the environment, we could reap the benefits of those natural resources we have. I must say that the Hon. Member on that side tries to raise some fears, perhaps, with his own experiences in the area. I honestly do not believe, Hon. Member, that you all have done a scientific analysis of what is available there. What we are trying to do there is to make a scientific analysis to find out whether there are any natural resources which are marketable and if so, the extent to which it could be used and whether it could be used to get us out of this economic rut. So, in that context, it is important that we do an exploration, not mining. The whole country is subject to exploration. In that case, Hon. Member, you will also not be happy with gem mining. There are areas where we do gem mining and I think there are quite long and deep excavations that take place. So, whether it is in the North, the South or the East is not what is important, but, if you have natural resources and if the country is really suffering economically, to ensure that we use those resources without impacting on the environment, and that is what we are doing. So, we will give you the assurance that for mining of whatever resources you say could affect

the environment in many ways, we will not issue licences. But, exploration of natural resources, I do not think you or anybody in this country could try to stop.
