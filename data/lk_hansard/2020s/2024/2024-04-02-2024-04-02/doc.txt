[12.28 p.m.] ( ) (The Hon. Rauff Hakeem, Attorney-at-Law) Hon. Deputy Speaker, we are discussing the Banking (Amendment) Bill that had been brought in order to regulate important areas of the banking business in this country, particularly at a time when we are faced with a serious debt crisis and are in the process of rescheduling our international debt. So, this Bill is very important at a time of this nature. We have, in the past, brought in the new Central Bank of Sri Lanka Bill. Now, this particular Banking (Amendment) Bill is an important step in the efforts to regulate the financial sector.

Sir, I would like to deal with one salient aspect of this Bill, which needs some clarity. In the Statement of Legal Effect of the Bill, referring to Clause 6, it is stated, I quote:

This clause amends section 5 of the principal enactment and the legal effect of the section as amended is to remove the reference to domestic banking business and offshore banking business considering the elimination of the demarcation between domestic banking business and the offshore banking business.

Then, referring to Clause 17, it is stated, I quote:

This clause repeals Part IV of the principal enactment and is consequential to the amendment made to section 5 of the principal enactment by Clause 6.

Sir, the clarity which I seek is this. Globally, offshore banking is a pivotal aspect of the international financial landscape, providing a nexus for global capital flow, fostering economic integration and enhancing the competitiveness of financial markets. In Sri Lanka, the initiation of offshore banking was done in 1979 under a stringent exchange control regime and was a strategic manoeuvre to attract foreign capital and leverage the countrys geographic and economic position as a regional financial hub. The incorporation of Offshore Banking Units or Foreign Currency Banking Units commonly referred to in banking circles to the legal framework via Part IV of the Banking Act of 1988 was a milestone in Sri Lankas financial history. It not only codified the practice, but also ensured a stable and a reliable environment for foreign investors, clearing the air of uncertainty and establishing a statutory basis for offshore financial operations. This legislative foresight has been fundamentally nurturing an ecosystem conducive to international trade and investment, reflecting our long-standing commitment to creating robust financial infrastructure. Sir, therefore, the proposition to repeal Part IV of the Banking Act through Clause 17 of the Banking (Amendment) Bill raises substantial concerns. This segment of the Act provided a clear statutory foundation for offshore banking crucial for maintaining investor confidence and financial stability. The repeal could reintroduce uncertainties inherent in administrative discretion, undermining the reliability and attractiveness of Sri Lanka as a financial hub, especially in these critical times when we are seeking to augment foreign investment through strategic initiatives like the Colombo Port City.

Sir, the regulatory action in 2023 by the Central Bank requiring businesses operating in the Colombo Port Citys offshore environment to operate through special accounts opened in Domestic Banking Units is intriguing as the ideal platform to operate offshore banking accounts was accounts opened in OBUs under Part IV of the Banking Act. The regulator complicated this landscape and introduced unnecessary risks and uncertainties by opting to permit these accounts under Domestic Banking Units and disregarding the existence of the more suitable offshore banking framework provided in Part IV of the Banking Act. Moreover, the replacement of statutory protections and administrative permissions could deter the current potential foreign entities due to the perceived increase in regulatory risk. This change may prompt a reassessment of the operational viability of banking accounts in Sri Lanka by businesses currently engaged in Offshore Banking Units, potentially leading to capital flight.

Therefore, Sir, I urge this esteemed House to consider this matter. If the proposed Amendments have been meticulously evaluated, it is imperative that the rationale along with the associated risks and benefits of repealing Part IV be transparently deliberated in this Parliament. The legislative process mandates thorough scrutiny of both the intents and implications of such significant Amendments to ensure that they contribute positively to our nations financial architecture and long-term economic vision.

Thank you.
