Hon. Presiding Member, I know that the Hon. Minister has been, for some time, seeking to legislate a multitude of enactments on many an aspect of the law and among such enactments, I would say, is this all-important Bill that is before us today, the Contempt of a Court, Tribunal or Institution Bill. The Bill eloquently states in its Long Title that it "......provides for the uniform application of the law relating to the contempt of a court, tribunal or Institution..." and also that it provides for the procedure to be followed. So, Sir, the purpose seems to be twofold: one is, to provide for a specific law on contempt and the other is, having provided for the law, to provide for the procedure to be followed in cases of contempt.

The question whether there should be a specific law of contempt has been around not only here, but in other jurisdictions as well. There was a controversy in India, but it was settled way back in 1971 through the Contempt of Courts Act of India. As one goes through the Supreme Court Determination in August, 2023 on this Bill, he could see that one of the main arguments in the Supreme Court had been that could you pass a specific legislation on contempt when there are constitutional provisions and even other enactments which penalize contempt of court? We know that Article 105(3) of the Constitution provides for contempt of superior courts. We also know that our Colleagues, the Hon. Ranjan Ramanayake and the Hon.

S.B. Dissanayake both, had to suffer the consequences of this power vested in the Supreme Court. The Hon. Ranjan Ramanayake, our former Colleague, said that 99 per cent of the judges in the country are corrupt and had to face incarceration for four years or more, which was subsequently reduced to a few years. Then, the Hon. S.B. Dissanayake had called one of the judgments as a balu theenduwa and was convicted and confined to a prison term of two years. Apart from the constitutional provision, there are other separate enactments that provide for contempt to be instituted against individuals. For instance, the Civil Procedure Code, the Code of Criminal Procedure and the Primary Courts Procedure Act. Then, we also have the Partition Law and the Judicature Act which provide for that. Those are a few examples where the provisions already exist. The argument made in the Court was that Parliament does not enjoy the legislative competence to enact a separate legislation on contempt, but the Supreme Court pointed out that the Reserved List preserves the right to pass a separate law on contempt. The Constitution, in the Reserved List - List II of the Ninth Schedule - under the heading Justice in so far as it relates to the judiciary and the courts structure very clearly provides for a subsequent enactment to deal with the contempt of court matters. This shows that as far back as the enactment of the Thirteenth Amendment to the Constitution, we had had this provision to make room for a new legislation in that regard.

With that controversy settled, Sir, the question arises as to what this law seeks to achieve. I have looked at it very carefully and would like to say that the offence of contempt has always been in a state of uncertainty; whilst some acts amounted to contempt, the others did not. We remember the old case where the editor of a newspaper was summoned and punished for contempt for writing about judges spending their time in Nuwara Eliya. He captioned that article Justice on Holiday and pointed out, in public spirit as he claimed, that judicial time was being wasted, but the judges of yesteryear would not let him go scot-free. I remember that case in the matter of Armand de Souza, the Editor of the "Ceylon Morning Leader" in 1914. It was reported in Volume 18 NLR 33. But, that all-important judgment recognized one important aspect of the law of contempt of court, which states, I quote:

There is no doubt as to the right of any member of the public to criticize, and to criticize strong, judicial decisions or judicial work, and to bring to the notice of the proper authorities any charge whatsoever of the alleged misconduct on the part of a judge.

So, the judges in 1914 pointed out that you can criticize a judgment, a judicial work and even misconduct, but to the proper authorities and that, however, you cannot comment on a personal holiday time that a judge is entitled to.

So, that being the case, this Bill now provides for some acts which would amount to contempt of court. For instance, it is set down in Clause 3(2)(a) that "willful disobedience to any judgment, decree, direction, order, writ or other process of a court...." will amount to contempt of court. Even a willful breach of an

327 328

2

undertaking given to a court will also become contemptuous under that Clause. So, what the Bill is seeking to do is to put a stututory footing to all that the judges had said in their decisions in the past. In other words, the common law principles that have been set down in judgments are being confined and codified, to some extent, in this Bill. We have no quarrel whatsoever as this legislation puts on a stututory footing the legal principles that had already been laid down by judges in this case.

Another aspect I would flag is what is meant by "scandalizing judicial authority", as that is sought to be made an offence. The tag scandalizing or lowering the judicial authority in Clause 3(2)(c)(i) of the Bill is, in my view, an unruly horse. Though the Supreme Court has affirmed its constitutional validity in the Bill, one cannot lose sight of the fact that the contours of "scandalization" are quite uncertain. The person who commits the contempt is left to the discretion and vagaries of the particular judge who hears his case. So, it is up to the adroit arguments of his counsel to take a particular act outside the ambit of scandalization. Therefore, there had been some criticisms even when this Bill was taken up on the last day. That is why the debate was adjourned for today. Therefore, we would like the Hon. Minister to have a revisit of the matter.

Sir, I was reading an interesting episode in Lord Dennings Book The Due Process of Law". In page 6, he narrates an interesting story that took place before him. It is worth quoting, Sir. Lord Denning states:

"...when I was presiding, we became more lenient. On every Monday morning we hear litigants in person. Miss Stone was often there. She made an application before us. We refused it. She was sitting in the front row with a bookcase within her reach. She picked up one of Butterworths Workmens Compensation Cases and threw it at us. It passed between Lord Justice Diplock and me. She picked up another. That went wide too. She said, 'I am running out of ammunition. We took little notice. She had hoped we would commit her for contempt of court - just to draw more attention to herself. As we took no notice, she went towards the door. She left saying: I congratulate your Lordships on your coolness under fire.' "

So, that is what Lord Dennings said of how magnanimous judges have been because it is not an issue for the ego of the judges to be punishing people for contempt of court.

Then, similarly, we have issues relating to matters that are raised in this House. We have the Parliament (Powers and Privileges) Act. Sometimes, some Hon. Members step outside the limits of criticizing a judgment in a proper sense, go beyond the ambit of criticism and take on judges and try to criticize their personal conduct and even recusal of judges. Recently, one Hon. Member of Parliament had gone beyond the limits. I had taken these issues up in the past also.

Sir, let me wind up by, once again, quoting Lord Denning. In R vs. Commissioner of Police (1968)2 QB 150, this is what he says:

Let me say at once that we will never use this jurisdiction as a means to uphold our own dignity. That must rest on surer foundations. Nor will we use it to suppress those who speak against us. We do not fear criticism, nor do we resent it. For there is something far more important at stake. It is no less than freedom of speech itself.

Though Lord Denning was magnanimous, this freedom cannot be the freedom of the wild ass. Article 15(2) of our Constitution specifically states that we cannot exercise the freedom of speech to commit contempt of court.

Having said that and quoted that, I would say that this was a long-felt need, to have proper contours defined as relate to contempt of court. I thank the Hon. Minister for having brought that Bill as well as the various other legislations to prevent frauds in this country, which are to be passed today. Therefore, I commend the Hon. Minister for having introduced these Bills.

Thank you. ( ) (The Hon. Presiding Member) . 2.
