( ) (The Hon. Presiding Member) Next, the Hon. M. Udayakumar. You have six minutes. [4.54 p.m.] . ( . ) (The Hon. M. Udayakumar) Hon. Presiding Member, the Adjournment Motion on the requirement for gender equality moved by the Hon. (Mrs.) Rohini Kumari Wijerathna is very vital at this point with the International Womens Day celebrated yesterday. Sir, gender equality is a human right; it is a necessary foundation for a peaceful and sustainable world and advancing gender equality is critical to all areas of a healthy society. Everyone deserves to be treated equally regardless of gender. With only a decade left to achieve the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development, it is crucial to ensure that every person has equal access to education, healthcare, decent work, a life free from discrimination and abuse and representation in political and economic decision-making.

Sri Lanka has made a significant progress towards achieving gender equality. However, in the face of structural barriers, many women and girls continue to suffer discrimination and from violence. Sir, at the same time, Sri Lanka is ranked well in several gender equality indexes compared to many other countries in the Region. However, a recent study by a Non-governmental Organization stated that 90 per cent of women in Sri Lanka are sexually harassed in public transport. Rape and sexual abuse cases are reported daily by the media and have been heard by courts in large numbers in recent days. So, I think we need to strengthen the law to eliminate such misbehaviours from our society.

Sir, the first actor in the trisector effort to encourage gender balance is the Government, which must build on ongoing efforts to bring more women into the workforce, particularly to the higher posts. In Sri Lanka, women account for only 34 per cent of the workforce, just below the Asia-Pacific average of 37 per cent and they contribute about 29 per cent to the national economy, one of the lowest participation rates in the region. Sir, to help address this, the Government introduced a quota for women in 2016 setting aside 25 per cent of the positions in Local Authorities for women, enhancing their representation in an effort to recognize the importance of women in socioeconomic development of Sri Lanka as well as promoting the need for greater participation of women in policy formulation.

Women constitute 52 per cent of Sri Lankas population, but the female representation in Parliament is only 5.3 per cent. Out of the 8.6 million economically active population, 65 per cent are males and only 35 per cent are females. Therefore, I must say that we should still focus on increasing the percentage of women's participation in politics, anything beyond the present allocation of 25 per cent. These initiatives by the

Government are important to help shift the social attitude on the role of women in society and work, protecting their rights and giving them an important place in the social and economic pyramid.

I would also like to say that companies have a big role to play in ensuring gender balance and Sri Lanka is progressing faster than many other countries in the Region on average. In the Asia-Pacific, about a quarter of managerial or higher positions are held by women while in Sri Lanka, the ratio has risen only to about one-third.

More importantly, I think education and awareness are crucial for this. Schools should consider ways of improving gender balance and work in tandem with companies to have sponsorships and mentoring programmes for women, encouraging them to participate more broadly in economic development. Such measures could encourage a change in attitude among policymakers, business people and the society. Generally, that is necessary to smooth the pathway towards gender parity. Once again, I would like to say that gender equality in Sri Lanka cannot be achieved without conscious efforts. If the tripartite actors - the Government, companies and the society - work together, progress can be made and everyone can capture the benefits of gender balance for the better.

Sir, in conclusion, I would suggest to this most valued House of Parliament to work out a strategy to find solutions to the many issues dealt with by the Hon. Members by forming a special Committee chaired by the respective Minister. I think, overall, promoting gender equality is important and necessary for the country in the long run.
