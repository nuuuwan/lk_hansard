Deputy Speaker, our country, unfortunately for the last 40 odd years, has been governed under Emergency Regulations. I think my generation in particular has lived under Emergency Regulations for so long, that for many of us it had become normalized. Certainly for the people of the North and the East, the Emergency Regulations were very much a part of their existence.

But, the Emergency Regulations imposed on the 18th of July, 2022, which we are debating today, are unique for two specific reasons. One is that they include measures which are draconian and most repressive in ways they have never been before. For instance, the Regulations allow a person to be arrested even by the military and to be kept up to 72 hours. I do not think this has happened before. The Regulations have also vested in the President powers which are largely unchecked. For 72 hours, a person arrested need not be presented before court or to the police. The powers that have been delegated to the

police and to the military have expanded exponentially. For example, simple offences are now described as high crimes and can be imposed with imprisonment up to 20 years or even life imprisonment. So, what is necessary is not to just look at the imposition of the Emergency Regulations, but at what has been gazetted under these particular Emergency Regulations since the measures that have been taken are unprecedented and they are unprecedented for a second reason: these measures were imposed at that time by an Acting President who has been unelected. President Ranil Wickremesinghe, as we know, is someone who lost his own constituency. It is he who came into power being elected to that position by this Parliament and is now imposing these draconian measures. So, when we are discussing these Emergency Regulations today, we have to take into account the specific nature of the Emergency measures, which are being considered and debated, and also the context within which these particular Regulations have been imposed.

In general, there is an understanding that for Emergency Regulations to be declared, there should be specific circumstances, that there should be some deep threat to national security or to public security. It is under those circumstances that Emergency Regulations are usually imposed. But, what are the conditions under which these Emergency Regulations have been imposed? We know that the protests that people have been engaging in for the past several months have arisen primarily due to the deep economic distress that people have been undergoing. Most of the protests started with the economic conditions that people have had to undergo and have been distressed by. How those kinds of protests can be described as a threat to national security and to public security is something that needs to be considered. People got on to the streets because of hunger, loss of livelihood, because their children have not been able to go to school, the shortage of medicines, fuel and the basic things that people need to get on with their lives. Those problems have still not been resolved; those problems are still there. Do you expect the people of this country to sit back and accept these conditions and to accept these as normal? It is not normal. They are not able to get on with their lives. Children have been deprived of school for over two and a half years. So, it is under those conditions that people got on to the streets, and the efforts to interpret and describe that as fascist and as actions of a terrorist group, to equate it in that way and to describe it in those terms show the lack of sensitivity to the concerns of the people and to what the people making such decisions are subject to.

Then, let us look at how these Emergency Regulations have been implemented. Was it necessary for the unarmed protestors to be evicted from their protest site at 1.00 a.m. on the 22nd of July, 2022 when they had already agreed to vacate that site on that day, within the next few hours. That was the agreement; they had announced they were leaving those sites. Yet, the Government saw it fit to attack those civilian protestors early in the morning of that day. They saw it fit to deny them access to legal support and medical support; ambulances were barred and lawyers were banned from entering those sites. Lawyers and media people who were there were also brutally attacked. How is this

proportionate to the seeming threat that the public or the nation was facing at that time? Since then, arrests of people who have been critical of the State and State institutions on social media have been made. How is that action proportionate to the threat to the nation? People have been pulled off planes. These sights are now being broadcast throughout the world in a way that is highly damaging to our international image and to the commitments this Government has made towards democracy and upholding the rights of the people. So, this is the reality where these Regulations are being experienced by the people. We know that Emergency Regulations come within the law of this country, that it is in our Constitution, but we also know that they have been used over and over again in this country to stifle dissent and criticism of the Government. It has always meant upholding the status quo and the establishment at the cost of civilian lives and their freedom.

Even in the Debate today, once again what we are seeing is that we are neglecting the causes of peoples protests, the reasons that have driven people to the streets and again, we are talking about the concerns of the 225 MPs here. Yes, what happened to the MPs - one lost his life and many lost their homes - is not something that could be condoned or is acceptable. But, over 20 civilians had also died in queues. So many citizens of our country have lost their homes on so many different occasions. But, have we been as concerned or as sensitive to those situations as we have been when our own lives and our own homes have been under attack? Is it not understandable that people have such a rage against the 225 of us and against this Parliament? Unless we take those feelings and experiences into account, we will continue to be despised; this institution will continue to be despised and seen as irrelevant to the lives of the people and that is highly dangerous to democracy in this country. So, think about what it is that we are trying to combat, whether this is proportionate to the situation that is outside, whether what is being implemented under the Emergency Regulations is proportionate to the threat that is perceived. At whom is this threat really being directed? Who is under threat? And, if you listen to the Debate today, you would understand that the people who are feeling most under threat are the 225 of us. So, does that warrant this kind of draconian intervention in order to safeguard the lives and property of 225 versus the millions outside and the concerns and issues that the millions outside are facing?
